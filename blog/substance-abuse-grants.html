Federal Grants for Substance Abuse Prevention and Treatment

Substance abuse treatment and prevention programs receive billions in federal funding annually to address the opioid crisis, support recovery, prevent youth substance use, and expand access to medication-assisted treatment (MAT). The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration (SAMHSA) is the primary federal funder, along with CDC, DOJ, and other agencies.

These grants support residential treatment facilities, outpatient programs, prevention coalitions, recovery community organizations, and harm reduction initiatives. Missing funding opportunities means fewer people receiving life-saving treatment.

Major Federal Substance Abuse Grant Programs

1. SAMHSA Substance Abuse Prevention and Treatment Block Grant

The SABG provides formula funding to states for substance abuse prevention and treatment services. States distribute funds to local providers. Typical subawards range from $100,000 to $2 million.

2. SAMHSA State Opioid Response (SOR) Grant

The SOR grant addresses the opioid crisis through prevention, treatment, and recovery support services. Awards to states range from $1 million to over $50 million annually, with funds distributed to local providers.

3. SAMHSA Tribal Opioid Response (TOR) Grant

The TOR program provides direct funding to tribal organizations to address opioid misuse in American Indian and Alaska Native communities. Awards range from $100,000 to $1 million.

4. SAMHSA Targeted Capacity Expansion (TCE) Grants

TCE grants help organizations expand substance abuse treatment capacity, including MAT and peer recovery support. Awards typically range from $300,000 to $500,000 annually.

5. CDC Overdose Data to Action (OD2A)

The CDC OD2A program funds states, territories, and localities to track drug overdoses and implement evidence-based prevention strategies. Awards range from $500,000 to $7 million.

Recent Opportunities

SAMHSA State Opioid Response Grant

Agency: Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration

Typical Award: $2,000,000 - $60,000,000 (to states; subgrants to providers)

Summary: Formula grants supporting prevention, treatment, and recovery services to address the opioid crisis. Funds medication-assisted treatment, overdose prevention, recovery housing, and peer support. Local providers receive funding through state agencies.

SAMHSA Substance Abuse Treatment for Individuals Experiencing Homelessness

Agency: Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration

Typical Award: $350,000 - $500,000/year

Summary: Supports outreach, screening, assessment, treatment, and recovery support services for individuals with substance use disorders experiencing or at risk of homelessness. Encourages harm reduction approaches.

SAMHSA Drug-Free Communities (DFC) Support Program

Agency: Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration

Typical Award: $125,000/year for up to 10 years

Summary: Funds community coalitions working to prevent youth substance use through environmental strategies, policy change, and community engagement. Supports coalition infrastructure and evidence-based prevention programming.

Who Is Eligible?

Substance abuse grant eligibility includes:

  • Substance abuse treatment facilities
  • 501(c)(3) nonprofit organizations
  • Community mental health centers
  • State and local substance abuse authorities
  • Community-based prevention coalitions
  • Recovery community organizations
  • Tribal health organizations
  • Healthcare providers offering MAT

What Types of Projects Get Funded?

Successful substance abuse grants typically support:

  • Medication-assisted treatment (MAT) - Buprenorphine, methadone, naltrexone programs
  • Residential treatment - Inpatient care, therapeutic communities
  • Outpatient treatment - Counseling, intensive outpatient programs
  • Prevention programs - School-based, community coalitions, media campaigns
  • Recovery support services - Peer support, recovery housing, case management
  • Harm reduction - Syringe services programs, naloxone distribution
  • Criminal justice diversion - Drug courts, treatment alternative to incarceration
  • Workforce development - Training for addiction counselors, peer specialists
